    Section 2.2 General overview of Earth’s atmospheric composition

    To understand how Earth’s atmosphere produces the wide variety of atmospheric conditions we interpret as weather and climate, we must know what the atmosphere is made of. Thus, we begin our detailed exploration of Earth’s atmosphere in this textbook with discussion of its composition.
    🔗
    The particles Earth’s atmosphere is composed of range in size from those that are too tiny to be seen individually by the naked eye—like the atoms and molecules that make up gases, the tiny liquid water droplets and solid ice crystals that constitute clouds, the lofted grains of dust that create dust storms, and the soot and ash particles that create smoke—to larger visible drops of rain, flakes of snow, pellets of sleet, and hail stones. These particles all are small, yet they control how Earth’s atmosphere interacts with sunlight and other energy flows; how it responds to and impacts underlying surfaces and living organisms on them; and ultimately how it creates weather and climate.
    🔗
    In summary, Earth’s atmosphere is composed largely of a mixture of gas particles—collectively called air—with much smaller amounts of suspended or falling liquid and solid particles dispersed unevenly through it. Depending on the application, we sometimes will focus on the behaviors of individual particles in this mixture, and at other times we will group many of these particles together and explore their bulk behavior.
    🔗
    The current composition of Earth’s atmosphere benefits our lives in many ways. A non-comprehensive list of these benefits includes the following.
    🔗
    • Air supplies dioxygen, which we and other living organisms breathe in to power cellular respiration.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Dioxygen allows for combustion, while dinitrogen limits combustion.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Plants and algae absorb carbon dioxide and release dioxygen during photosynthesis.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Gases allow insects, birds, and bats as well as aircraft to fly.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Gases further enable travel by pushing against the sails of sailboats.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Gases enable sound waves to propagate.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Gases cause meteors and other space debris to burn up before reaching Earth’s surface.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Gases reflect and absorb dangerous high-energy radiation and particles produced by the Sun and other celestial sources.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Gases exert pressure, which allows liquid water to exist on Earth’s surface.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Most importantly for this textbook, the current composition of Earth’s atmosphere creates the weather we experience and the climate we’re used to through various processes. A non-comprehensive list of these processes includes the following.
    🔗
    • Different particles interact with solar and terrestrial radiation in different ways, thereby giving us beautiful colors in the sky and livable temperatures on Earth’s surface.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Some gases absorb harmful high-energy solar radiation before it can reach Earth’s surface, thereby protecting our health and the health of other living organisms while warming the atmosphere.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Water vapor, which is the gaseous form of water, condenses into liquid water drops and deposits into ice crystals to create clouds and precipitation as well as dew and frost.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The movement of gases through the atmosphere lessens temperature extremes and brings moisture to even far inland locations.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Without the atmosphere, life as we know it could not exist on Earth. You can look to Earth’s uninhabitable Moon for an example of what conditions on a celestial body with essentially no atmosphere are like!
    🔗
